Gradient descent — always downhill, sometimes forever

The gradient ∇f points in the direction of steepest ascent, so stepping the opposite way, x ← x − η∇f, always heads downhill. It's dead simple, but the fixed step size η is a tyrant: too small and it crawls, too large and it overshoots and diverges. In a curved valley like Rosenbrock's it zig-zags across the walls and inches along the floor — the textbook illustration of why plain gradient descent is slow. On Rastrigin it simply falls into the nearest dimple and stops, never seeing the global minimum.

Momentum — a heavy ball with memory

Instead of stepping by the raw gradient, accumulate a velocity: v ← βv − η∇f, then x ← x + v. Consistent downhill directions add up so the ball accelerates along the valley floor, while the oscillations that stall gradient descent partly cancel out. It reaches the bottom of Rosenbrock far sooner than plain descent — at the cost of sometimes overshooting and rolling past the minimum before settling. Here β = 0.85 and the step size is matched so you can compare it fairly against gradient descent.

Nelder–Mead simplex — downhill without a derivative

The "amoeba" keeps a triangle of three points (a simplex) and never touches the gradient. Each step it reflects its worst vertex through the opposite edge; if that lands somewhere great it expands further, if it's still bad it contracts inward, and if all else fails it shrinks toward the best vertex. The triangle tumbles, stretches, and squeezes its way downhill — robust when you have no derivatives or a noisy function, though it can stall on hard curved valleys.
