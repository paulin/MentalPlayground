The Fast Fourier Transform

Every signal is secretly a sum of sine waves. The FFT is the algorithm that takes a wiggling waveform (top) and tells you exactly which frequencies it's made of (bottom) — in N log N time instead of N². Build a signal on the left and watch the transform pick it apart.

Real-world knobs

The FFT assumes your slice repeats forever. When it doesn't line up, energy smears across bins (spectral leakage). A window tapers the edges to tame it. Set a frequency to a non-integer-looking value and toggle this to see the peaks tighten.

What you're looking at

The top plot is a signal sampled at 512 evenly spaced instants. The bottom plot is its magnitude spectrum: bin k tells you how much of a sine wave that completes k full cycles across the window is present. A pure tone shows up as a single spike; add noise and the noise floor rises evenly everywhere while the spikes stay put — which is exactly why the FFT is how you pull a signal out of noise.

Why "fast"

The Discrete Fourier Transform is N² multiply-adds done naively. The Cooley–Tukey FFT notices the DFT of an N-point signal can be built from two DFTs of its even- and odd-indexed halves, recursively — collapsing the cost to N log N. At N=1024 that's the difference between ~1,000,000 and ~10,000 operations. This page runs a real radix-2 Cooley–Tukey transform in your browser on every slider move.
