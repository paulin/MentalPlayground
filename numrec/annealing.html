Simulated Annealing

Metallurgists cool metal slowly so its atoms settle into a low-energy crystal; anneal too fast and you freeze in defects. Simulated annealing borrows the trick to escape bad local optima: early on, when the temperature is high, it will accept moves that make things worse — then, as it cools, it grows pickier until it locks in. Here it untangles a traveling-salesman tour: watch the crossings vanish as the temperature falls.

The Metropolis rule: sometimes accept a worse move

The "energy" here is the total tour length. Each step proposes a 2-opt move — cut two edges and reconnect them the other way, which reverses a stretch of the route and removes a crossing. If the move shortens the tour (ΔE < 0) it's always accepted. If it lengthens it, it's still accepted with probability exp(−ΔE / T). When T is high that probability is near 1, so the search roams freely and climbs out of local minima; as T → 0 it vanishes and the method becomes pure greedy descent.

The cooling rate α is everything. Each temperature step multiplies T by α (here geometric cooling). Cool slowly — α near 1 — and the tour has time to reorganize into something close to optimal; cool too fast and it freezes with crossings still in it, the algorithmic version of a brittle, badly-annealed casting. Nudge α up and down and watch the final tour quality change. Numerical Recipes presents exactly this traveling-salesman example in its Simulated Annealing Methods section.
