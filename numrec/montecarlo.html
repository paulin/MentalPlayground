Monte Carlo & Random Numbers

You can compute a lot by simply guessing at random and keeping score. Throw darts to measure π, estimate an integral by counting hits under a curve, or turn a stream of uniform noise into any distribution you like. Along the way, see why error shrinks like 1/√N — and how quasi-random points that deliberately avoid clustering do dramatically better.

Estimating π by throwing darts

Scatter points uniformly over a unit square and ask how many land inside the quarter circle x² + y² ≤ 1. That fraction is the ratio of areas — (π/4) / 1 — so π ≈ 4 × inside / total. It's the simplest Monte Carlo estimate there is, and a perfect illustration of the method's blessing and curse: the answer is unbiased, but the statistical error only falls as 1/√N. To squeeze out one more decimal digit you need 100× more darts. Watch the estimate jitter toward 3.14159 and never quite settle.

Monte Carlo integration — hit or miss

To integrate f(x), box it in and throw darts at the box: the fraction landing under the curve, times the box's area, estimates the integral. No quadrature rule, no evenly-spaced panels — just counting. In one dimension this is far slower than Simpson's rule, but Monte Carlo's error scales as 1/√N regardless of dimension, so in high dimensions — where grid methods need an astronomical number of points — it wins outright. That's why it dominates in statistical physics and finance.

Turning uniform noise into any distribution

A raw generator only gives you uniform numbers on [0, 1). Everything else is built from them. The transformation method (§7.2) feeds a uniform value through the inverse of the target's cumulative distribution — that's how the exponential deviate x = −ln u is born, and how the Box–Muller formula spins two uniforms into two independent Gaussians. When the inverse CDF is intractable, the rejection method (§7.3) throws darts under the density curve and simply keeps the ones that land below it. Watch the histogram fill in and lock onto the smooth target curve.
