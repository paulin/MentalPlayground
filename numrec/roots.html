Bisection — slow but unstoppable

Start with two points that straddle a root, so f(a) and f(b) have opposite signs. Evaluate the midpoint and keep whichever half still contains the sign change. The bracket halves every step, so the error shrinks by exactly a factor of 2 each iteration — linear convergence. It's guaranteed to converge but needs ~3.3 iterations per decimal digit. If the two handles don't straddle a root, bisection can't even start.

Newton–Raphson — fast, when it behaves

From a single guess, follow the tangent line down to where it hits the axis; that's your next guess. Near a simple root the number of correct digits roughly doubles each step — quadratic convergence. The catch: it needs the derivative, and a bad starting point can send it flying off or trap it in a cycle. Try the x³ − 2x + 2 function starting near 0 to watch Newton get stuck bouncing between two points forever.

Secant — Newton without the derivative

Replace Newton's tangent with a chord (secant line) through the two most recent points — no derivative required. Convergence is superlinear, order ≈ 1.618 (the golden ratio): slower than Newton per step, but each step is cheaper because it skips the derivative evaluation. It shares Newton's fragility on bad guesses.
